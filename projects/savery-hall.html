The Challenge

Savery Hall is one of the University of Washington's most architecturally distinctive buildings, with a complex Gothic Revival facade featuring intricate window patterns, ornamental stonework, and layered depth. The challenge was to create a accurate parametric digital twin that could capture these details while remaining fully editable.

Traditional static modeling would have been faster, but the goal was to build something that could adapt — if a window dimension changed, the entire facade would update. This required a deep understanding of parametric constraints and relationship-driven design.

Skills Developed

Fusion 360 Parametric Constraints Facade Detailing Measurement & Survey Design Intent

Design Process

Research & Measurement

Conducted on-site measurements and photographic documentation of Savery Hall. Referenced architectural blueprints and historical records to capture proportions accurately.

Structural Framework

Built the foundational structure using parametric sketches, establishing key reference planes and dimensional relationships that would drive the entire model.

Facade Detailing

Modeled repeated window patterns using rectangular patterns and mirroring operations. Each element was linked to master dimensions so changes would propagate automatically.

Refinement & Rendering

Added material assignments, fine-tuned surface details, and created presentation renders to showcase the model from multiple viewpoints.

What I Learned

Parametric thinking is a different discipline. It's not just about creating geometry — it's about encoding intent. Every sketch relationship is a decision about what should change together and what shouldn't.

Accuracy demands patience. The gap between "looks right" and "is right" became clear when dimensions were off by millimeters. Building in measurement-first habits early saved hours of rework.

Complexity needs hierarchy. Without organized component groups and clear naming conventions, the model quickly became unmanageable. This project taught me the value of treating CAD projects like software — structured, documented, and modular.
