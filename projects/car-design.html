The Challenge

Automotive surface design demands a different kind of precision than architectural modeling. Surfaces need to flow seamlessly across panels, maintaining G2 continuity while respecting manufacturing constraints. The challenge was to design a vehicle body that was both aesthetically compelling and geometrically sound.

Working in OnShape's collaborative environment added another layer — version control, branching, and real-time co-editing required a disciplined approach to model organization that mirrors software development workflows.

Skills Developed

Design Process

Reference & Inspiration

Studied contemporary automotive designs and aerodynamic principles. Created mood boards and side-profile sketches to establish proportions and design language before touching CAD.

Profile Sketches & Splines

Developed key cross-section profiles using carefully controlled splines. These profiles defined the car's silhouette from multiple angles and would drive the surface lofts.

Surface Lofting & Assembly

Used multi-section lofts and boundary surfaces to create the main body panels. Ensured tangent continuity between panels, then assembled the chassis structure underneath.

Detailing & Presentation

Added wheel arches, headlight recesses, air intakes, and panel split lines. Created exploded assembly views to showcase internal structure and component relationships.

What I Learned

Surface quality is everything. In solid modeling you can get away with approximate geometry. With surfaces, even tiny curvature discontinuities create visible reflections lines that ruin the design. This project sharpened my eye for geometric quality.

Collaboration requires structure. OnShape's branching model is powerful, but without clear naming conventions and a merge strategy, parallel work quickly creates conflicts. I learned to treat CAD collaboration like git — commit often, describe changes, merge deliberately.

Aesthetic judgment is a technical skill. Evaluating whether a surface "looks right" required developing an intuition for curvature continuity — blending art and engineering in a way I hadn't experienced before.
